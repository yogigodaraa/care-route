# Problem 03: Where to Go When — Patient Care Decision Support

**Domain:** Healthcare · Consumer Health

**Tags:** Decision Support, Consumer-Facing, Agentic AI

**Difficulty:** Medium

**Suggested Team:** Full-stack + UX + product thinker

**Data:** Government symptom checker data (public), urgent care and GP directory data (public), WA Health "Where to Go When" website

---

## The Problem

Emergency departments are full of people who didn't need to be there. A broken arm, a persistent cough, a sick child — conditions that could be treated faster and better at a GP, urgent care clinic, or pharmacy. The moment a patient walks through ED doors and gets triaged, the system is obligated to treat them regardless. State governments run awareness campaigns, but no tool exists in a patient's hands at the moment they're actually making the decision — usually stressed, often with a screaming kid in tow. Build something that gets to people before they get to ED.

---

## Potential Directions

- Mobile-first symptom-to-care-setting decision tool that guides users to the right place based on what they're experiencing
- Integration with existing government symptom checkers and real-time GP and urgent care availability nearby
- Business model built around diversion value — monetising the routing of patients to the right provider, not just the information
- Embeddable widget for state government health apps or GP and urgent care clinic websites

---

## Considerations

- **Crisis behaviour is irrational:** When someone is panicked — sick child, sudden injury, middle of the night — they default to the most familiar and trusted option. That's usually 000 or the nearest ED. Any solution that requires a calm, considered decision to open an app and work through a flow will fail at the moment it's needed most. Think about how you reach people before the crisis, not during it.

- **Trust is everything in health:** People are making decisions about their body or their child's body. A clunky, generic, or unconfident tool will be ignored. The product has to feel authoritative and simple — not like a government FAQ page.

- **This is a distribution problem as much as a product problem:** A perfect decision tool that lives on a government website nobody visits solves nothing. Think hard about where people actually are when they need this — Google search, a GP's website, a pharmacy app, a parenting community — and how the product gets there.

- **Liability and scope creep are real constraints:** The moment a product starts feeling like a diagnostic tool, it enters heavily regulated territory. The strongest solutions stay firmly on the side of directing people to the right care setting — not telling them what's wrong with them.

- **Behaviour change is slow; habit formation is the goal:** The most durable version of this product is one people encounter before they need it — during a GP visit, during pregnancy, when they first move to a new suburb — so that when the moment comes, the instinct is already there.

---

## Potential Customers

- State health departments and private insurers looking to reduce avoidable ED presentations
- Parents of young children making care decisions under stress, outside business hours
